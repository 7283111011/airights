Scott McCulloch

Founder, FAIR

M.S. Psychology and Neuroscience, King’s College London · LLB candidate, University of Law · Postgraduate study in AI Law, University of Oxford · Intellectual Property, Brunel University London

Why I started FAIR

Most of my career, I have supported drug development by helping organisations navigate complex regulatory requirements, ensuring patient safety and rights are upheld whilst generating data of the highest integrity.—Scott McCulloch

Background

For close to two decades, Scott McCulloch has advised drug-development organisations on the regulatory frameworks designed to protect patients in clinical trials. His work has spanned sponsors, contract research organisations and service providers running studies in Europe, the Americas and Asia-Pacific. The job, simply put, was to make sure the data submitted to regulators - the FDA, the MHRA, the EMA and others - is verifiable and stands up to inspection. Compliance, in that world, is not aspirational; it is the difference between a medicine reaching the public and not reaching the public.

That experience - what enforceable regulation actually looks like, what it costs organisations to comply with, and what it does for the people it is supposed to protect - is what he brings to FAIR.

The turn to law

Scott has more recently turned to the law. He is in the final year of an LLB at the University of Law and is preparing for admission as a practising solicitor in England and Wales. Alongside the LLB he is reading postgraduate programmes in AI Law at the University of Oxford and Intellectual Property at Brunel University London. He holds an MSc in Psychology and Neuroscience from King’s College London.

What sparked FAIR

The immediate catalyst for FAIR was Scott’s final research project on the LLB at the University of Law. The project examined whether United Kingdom intellectual property law had been adapted to two parallel challenges: the implementation of artificial intelligence by organisations across the public and private sectors, and the protection of the creators whose work increasingly trains those systems. The conclusion, on both counts, was that it had not. From that finding came the conviction that a dedicated organisation was needed, with a remit to help people, in their personal and professional lives, navigate the artificial intelligence now being deployed around them.

Why FAIR, and why now

FAIR was set up because the UK is, in his view, mid-way through deploying AI across public services - healthcare, welfare, immigration, policing, schools - faster than the rules to govern it. The Government’s promised AI Bill has been quietly dropped. The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 made it easier, not harder, for organisations to make automated decisions about people. And the most affected groups - patients, benefits claimants, parents, school pupils, working creators - are not the groups currently being consulted.

FAIR’s role, as Scott sees it, is to apply the same approach to AI that pharmaceutical regulation has long applied to medicines: enforceable standards, not voluntary commitments; transparency by default; a meaningful appeal route when something goes wrong; and ordinary people being told, in plain English, what is being done to them and what their rights are.
