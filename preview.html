Right now in the United Kingdom, artificial intelligence is being used to decide whether you get a benefit payment, whether your face matches a police watchlist, whether your child’s coursework is genuine, whether your CV makes the shortlist, whether your X-ray gets a second look, and whether your doctor can spend more time with you or less.

Most of this is happening without anyone asking you, and often without anyone telling you. FAIR exists because British citizens are being asked to live inside a system they did not consent to and were not taught to navigate. Our job is to help you navigate it, and to keep the pressure on the people who can change it.

Where the law sits today

The legal framework around artificial intelligence is moving, slowly, and not always in the public’s favour. These are the four events of the last twelve months that matter most.

In force 5 February 2026
Live

Data (Use and Access) Act 2025

Made it easier, not harder, for organisations to make solely automated decisions about people. Articles 22A to 22D set the new defaults.

Read at legislation.gov.uk →
Quietly dropped, 2025
Dropped

Government AI Bill

The promised comprehensive AI Bill was shelved with no replacement timetable. The United Kingdom has no horizontal AI statute as of today.

Read background →
Royal Assent 2026
In force

Crime and Policing Act 2026

Banned the apps that strip clothes off photographs of women and children. A real win. Enforcement, however, is only as good as the agencies behind it.

Read at gov.uk →

After a year-long consultation in which 88% of online respondents asked Government to require AI companies to license copyrighted work, ministers postponed the decision again.

Research and Publications

Nine areas where United Kingdom research is unambiguous, regulators are concerned, and the public has not yet been given clear information. Pick an area on the left to read the evidence in detail.

Health01

In the NHS

The NHS Federated Data Platform, built by Palantir under a contract worth up to £330 million awarded in November 2023, is being rolled out across up to 240 trusts. A contract break-clause review is reported for spring 2027. The British Medical Association voted to oppose the rollout at its June 2025 representatives’ meeting. The MHRA’s National Commission into the Regulation of AI in Healthcare closed its call for evidence on 2 February 2026 and is due to report in summer 2026. An Oxford-led study (Nature Medicine, February 2026) of 1,300 UK participants found large language models do not improve self-diagnosis accuracy and frequently produce unsafe recommendations.

What this means for you: artificial intelligence is being put into NHS systems faster than the rules to govern it.
Back the healthcare AI disclosure campaign →
Welfare02

At the DWP

The Universal Credit Advances machine-learning model (operational since 2022) was confirmed by a DWP fairness analysis, released to the Public Law Project under FOI in late 2024, to show statistically significant referral and outcome disparity across age, disability, marital status and nationality. The National Audit Office and Public Accounts Committee have repeatedly flagged the lack of transparency. As of April 2025, DWP was still “finalising publication” of its algorithmic transparency records.

What this means for you: an algorithm may be deciding whether your benefits get paid, with no explanation and no proper appeal route.
Know your rights on automated decisions →
Immigration03

At the Home Office

The Identify and Prioritise Immigration Cases (IPIC) algorithm, in use since 2019 to 2020, processes the cases of around 41,000 people facing potential removal. Caseworkers click “accept” with no explanation required; rejections require written justification. Privacy International filed an ICO complaint on 18 August 2025 and an ICO investigation is open. A facial age estimation system for asylum seekers is being trialled.

What this means for you: the workflow nudges decision-makers towards the algorithm’s answer rather than independent judgement.
Report your experience →
Policing04

In policing

The Metropolitan Police’s live facial recognition deployments led to 962 arrests between September 2024 and September 2025, with over 1,400 cumulative arrests since LFR was scaled up. The High Court has ruled the Met’s use lawful, but Black and Asian respondents to the Ada / Turing Wave 2 survey were significantly more concerned about police facial recognition (57% and 52%) than the general population (39%).

What this means for you: who counts as a match depends partly on how the system was trained, and the burden of being wrongly stopped does not fall evenly across the country.
Know your rights about police data →
Children05

In children’s lives

Ofcom’s Online Nation 2025 (10 December 2025) found 79% of UK online 13 to 17 year olds, and 40% of 7 to 12 year olds, now use generative AI; Snapchat My AI is the most-used by children at 51% of online 7 to 17s; ChatGPT registered 1.8 billion UK visits in the first eight months of 2025. The Internet Watch Foundation logged a 26,385% year-on-year rise in AI-generated child sexual abuse videos in 2025 (3,443, up from 13 in 2024). The Crime and Policing Act 2026 has criminalised the supply of nudification tools, but possession-side gaps remain.

What this means for you: most parents and carers do not know how their children are using artificial intelligence, and the tools to harm those children are evolving faster than the laws to stop it.
Read the parents’ guide →
Schools06

In schools

The National Education Union’s 2026 State of Education survey of 9,408 teachers found 76% now use AI for daily work, 49% of schools have no AI policy at all, and 66% of secondary teachers say pupils’ critical thinking has declined because of AI. The National Literacy Trust’s 2025 survey of more than 60,000 13 to 18 year olds found 1 in 4 (25.1%) admitted to “just copying” AI outputs into homework, up from 20.9% in 2024.

What this means for you: schools are using artificial intelligence without consistent rules, and the skills that qualifications are supposed to certify are being eroded.
Read the secondary-age guide →
Universities07

In universities

The Russell Group’s five principles (2023, refreshed 2024 to 2025) and JCQ’s April 2025 guidance set the rules. The Kosmyna et al. MIT Media Lab study (June 2025, Your Brain on ChatGPT) used EEG to track 54 participants writing essays with and without LLM help, and reported significantly weaker brain connectivity and “cognitive debt” in heavy LLM users. Caveat: arXiv preprint, not yet peer-reviewed.

What this means for you: heavy AI use during learning may carry cognitive costs we do not yet fully understand.
Read the students’ guide →
Creative08

In creative work

United Kingdom creative industries generated £146 billion in GVA in 2024 (around 6% of the economy). 11,500+ responses to the December 2024 to February 2025 Government copyright consultation included 88% (10,112 of online respondents) backing licensing in all cases, and only 3% backing the Government’s preferred opt-out option. The 18 March 2026 Written Ministerial Statement formally dropped the opt-out and announced four follow-up workstreams. The High Court’s judgment in Getty Images v Stability AI [2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch) was the first United Kingdom ruling on generative AI and copyright; it left the central training-data question open and effectively passed it to Parliament.

What this means for you: your creative work may be training artificial intelligence right now, and the law has not yet caught up.
Back the licensing-first campaign →
Law09

Human in the loop

The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025’s new UK GDPR Article 22A (in force 5 February 2026) ties the whole automated-decision regime to whether there is “meaningful human involvement”, a term the Act does not define. The Secretary of State is empowered (Article 22D) to define it by secondary legislation. Until that happens, every United Kingdom automated-decision system in the public and private sector sits in a definitional grey area.

What this means for you: even after February 2026, the rules on whether a human has to actually consider your case are still being written.
Back the meaningful-human-involvement campaign →

Scott McCulloch
Founder, FAIR
“Most of my career, I have supported drug development by helping organisations navigate complex regulatory requirements, ensuring patient safety and rights are upheld whilst generating data of the highest integrity.”

Scott has spent close to two decades advising drug-development organisations on the regulatory frameworks designed to protect patients in clinical trials. He is in the final stages of an LLB at the University of Law, with postgraduate study in AI Law at the University of Oxford and Intellectual Property at Brunel University London. The immediate catalyst for FAIR was his final research project on the LLB, which examined whether lectual property law had been adapted to the use of artificial intelligence by organisations and to the protection of the creators whose work increasingly trains those systems. The conclusion, on both counts, was that it had not. FAIR is the organisation he set up in response, on the conviction that the British public deserves the same rigour of protection from artificial intelligence that we have long given to medicines, and because the people most affected by these systems in this country are not the people currently being asked.
