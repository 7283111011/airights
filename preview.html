AI systems are being deployed at scale across healthcare, education, financial services, and the wider public sector, yet the UK has, to date, declined to introduce comprehensive statutory safeguards.

No domestic legislation imposes binding transparency obligations upon AI developers. There exists no independent audit framework, no statutory duty of disclosure as to training data, system instructions, or commercial arrangements influencing model outputs, and no proper redress for individuals subject to opaque automated decisions. The AI (Regulation) Bill, having progressed through earlier parliamentary stages, remains stalled. Successive Governments have deferred substantive action.

FAIR was founded to put forward, on the basis of evidence and direct engagement with Parliament, the case for enforceable rights and proper protections in this area.

Pharmaceuticals are regulated by the MHRA. Aviation by the CAA. Financial services by the FCA. AI, in the United Kingdom, has no comparable regulator, and no statute that does the same job.

FAIR's role is to bring evidence and analysis to the people who can change that, in Parliament, in the regulators, and in the wider professional community working on AI law and policy.

Rights & public education

Advancing public understanding of the rights engaged by AI, from the protection of creative works and personal data to safeguards against opaque automated decisions, and the legal frameworks in which those rights operate.

Policy & regulatory engagement

Providing research, technical briefings, and written evidence to Select Committees, regulators, and Parliamentarians, so that decisions on AI policy and legislation are informed by independent, evidence-based analysis.

Education & public accountability

Evidence-based AI literacy work, plain-language briefings, and monitoring AI developments across Westminster, Whitehall, and the regulatory bodies.

Documented Concerns

What the evidence shows

Described below are findings drawn from peer reviewed research, parliamentary inquiries, regulatory bodies, and professional surveys published over the last eighteen months. Each illustrates a specific harm, gap, or risk to which current law offers no adequate response.

Healthcare

NHS clinicians have raised concerns that opaque AI diagnostic tools trained on non-representative datasets risk misdiagnosing patients from ethnic minorities, women, and older populations.

Nouis, Uren and Jariwala, BMC Medical Ethics, 2025
Read the study →
Education

Only 9% of UK teachers feel confident teaching AI, yet 76% already use it for daily work. 49% of schools have no AI policy at all. Two thirds of secondary teachers report declining critical thinking among pupils.

NEU Survey, Feb 2026; Pearson School Report, Dec 2025
Read the findings →
Child Safety

Ofcom fined an AI nudification site operator in November 2025 and opened investigations into AI chatbot services in January 2026. The proposed KCSIE 2026 guidance formally recognises AI generated abuse material as a safeguarding concern.

Ofcom; DfE KCSIE 2026 Consultation
Read the inquiry →
Transparency

Research presented at FAccT 2025 and CHI 2026 found that hidden system prompts in AI products introduce systematic bias without users knowledge. Most providers actively instruct their models to conceal these instructions from the public.

Neumann et al., FAccT 2025; CHI 2026
Read more →
Financial Services

The Bank of England Financial Policy Committee has flagged systemic risk from growing AI use in trading, credit scoring, and operational functions, but no AI specific financial rules exist in the UK.

Bank of England and PRA, April 2026
Read more →
Copyright

The House of Lords described the creative industries as facing a clear and present danger from generative AI. The government March 2026 report acknowledged that no consensus exists on its own objectives.

Lords Communications Committee, March 2026

Priority Areas

Where we are focusing our work

The following areas have been identified, through evidence drawn from research, regulatory commentary, and parliamentary inquiry, as priorities for FAIR’s policy and advocacy programme. Additional priorities may be added on formation of the Board.

These remain proposed projects, subject to consultation with and approval by the Board.

AI Copyright and Intellectual Property Reform

A balanced commercial TDM exception with mandatory disclosure, producer rights reform, and a market access provision for models trained outside the UK.

View project →

AI and Child Safety

Comprehensive age gating, content provenance standards, and criminal liability for platform operators of nudification, deepfake, and AI companion services.

View project →

Public Education and AI Literacy

Evidence-based educational programmes and plain-language briefings on every significant AI policy development before Parliament.

View project →

AI Transparency and Accountability

Statutory disclosure obligations covering training data, system instructions, and any commercial arrangements that influence AI outputs.

View project →

Parliamentary Advocacy and Legislative Drafting

Direct engagement with Parliamentarians, expert evidence to Select Committees, model provisions, and cross party work to shape the forthcoming AI Bill.

View project →

Why I Started FAIR
“I’ve spent most of my career making sure people in clinical trials are protected by enforceable rules. AI should be held to the same standard.”
Scott McCulloch
Founder · Founding Member

Scott McCulloch is the founder of FAIR. For nearly two decades he has worked on the regulation of clinical trials, with sponsors and CROs running studies across Europe, the Americas, and Asia-Pacific. He is in the final year of an LLB at the University of Law, with concurrent postgraduate work in AI Law at Oxford and in Intellectual Property at Brunel University London. He founded FAIR because he believes AI should be governed with the same rigour as medicines.

Scott McCulloch

Founder, FAIR

M.S. Psychology and Neuroscience, King’s College London · LLB, University of Law

Scott McCulloch has spent nearly two decades working on the regulation of clinical trials. He has advised sponsors and contract research organisations on studies run across Europe, the Americas, and Asia-Pacific, covering the full development lifecycle from first-in-human studies through to post-marketing commitments. The work is built on making sure the data submitted to regulators (the FDA, the MHRA, the EMA, and others) is verifiable and stands up to inspection.

What he brings to FAIR is that experience: knowing first-hand what real, enforceable regulation looks like, what it costs organisations to comply with, and what it actually does for the people it is supposed to protect.

He has more recently turned to the law. He is in the final year of an LLB at the University of Law and is preparing for admission as a practising solicitor. Alongside the LLB he is reading postgraduate programmes in AI Law at Oxford and in Intellectual Property at Brunel University London. He holds an MSc in Psychology and Neuroscience from King’s College London.

FAIR exists, in his view, to apply the same approach to artificial intelligence that he has applied to clinical trials: enforceable standards, not voluntary commitments.
