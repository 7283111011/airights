FAIR — The Foundation for AI Rights

AI is being built into the country around you. We’re here to help you understand it, push back on it, and demand it works for you.

FAIR is a new UK organisation with two jobs. The first is to help people — children, teens, students, working adults, older adults, families and carers — actually understand what AI is, what it can and can’t do, and what your rights are when a public service, school, employer or app uses it on you. The second is to hold the people building and buying these systems to account: in Whitehall, in Parliament, in regulators’ offices, in NHS trusts, in council chambers, and in police forces.

What FAIR is for

Right now in the UK, AI is being used to decide whether you get a benefit payment, whether your face matches a police watchlist, whether your child’s coursework is genuine, whether your CV makes the shortlist, whether your X-ray gets a second look, and whether your doctor can spend more time with you or less.

Most of this is happening without anyone asking you, and often without anyone telling you.

The law is moving — but slowly, and not always in your favour. The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 came into force on 5 February 2026 and made it easier, not harder, for organisations to make automated decisions about you. The promised Government AI Bill has been quietly dropped. The Crime and Policing Act 2026 has banned the apps that strip clothes off photographs of women and children — a real win — but enforcement is only as good as the agencies behind it. And after a year-long consultation in which 88% of online respondents asked the Government to require AI companies to license copyrighted work, ministers postponed the decision again in March 2026.

FAIR exists because UK citizens are being asked to live inside a system they did not consent to and were not taught to navigate. Our job is to help you navigate it — and to keep the pressure on the people who can change it.

What we do

Five jobs, equal weight

Medicines have the MHRA. Aviation has the CAA. Banks have the FCA. AI in the UK has none of these — yet it is now used in your hospital, your school, your benefits claim, your court file, and your local police force. FAIR exists to fill that gap from both ends: helping the public understand and use their rights, and pushing the people in charge to write better ones.

1
Learn

Age-tiered AI education for the British public — primary children, teens, students, working adults, parents, and older adults. Free, plain English, mapped to the UK curriculum.
2
Watch

A public register of how UK public services are using AI. Every contract, pilot, partnership and algorithmic system in the NHS, DWP, Home Office, MoJ, councils, schools and police forces — sourced from official records.
3
Report

A safe channel for people to tell us what AI is actually doing to them. We collate, anonymise and use what you tell us in our research, our briefings and our campaigns.
4
Campaign

Take action on the things that need to change now. Specific, time-bound campaigns on the issues with the clearest evidence and the strongest public mandate.
5
Engage

Independent, evidence-based work with Parliament and regulators. Written evidence to Select Committees, briefings to Peers and MPs, and submissions to the ICO, Ofcom, MHRA, CMA, FCA, Ofqual and the AI Security Institute.
What’s actually going wrong

Where the evidence is loudest right now

These are the areas where UK research is unambiguous, regulators are concerned, and the public has not yet been given clear information. Each one is sourced. Click through to read the original.

  1. 01
    Healthcare

    NHS clinicians have raised concerns that opaque AI diagnostic tools trained on non-representative datasets risk misdiagnosing patients from ethnic minorities, women, and older populations.

    Nouis, Uren and Jariwala, BMC Medical Ethics, 2025. Read the study →
  2. 02
    Education

    Only 9% of UK teachers feel confident teaching AI, yet 76% already use it for daily work. 49% of schools have no AI policy at all. Two-thirds of secondary teachers report declining critical thinking among pupils.

    NEU Survey, Feb 2026; Pearson School Report, Dec 2025. Read the findings →
  3. 03
    Child Safety

    Ofcom fined an AI nudification site operator in November 2025 and opened investigations into AI chatbot services in January 2026. The proposed KCSIE 2026 guidance formally recognises AI-generated abuse material as a safeguarding concern.

    Ofcom; DfE KCSIE 2026 Consultation. Read the inquiry →
  4. 04
    Transparency

    Research presented at FAccT 2025 and CHI 2026 found that hidden system prompts in AI products introduce systematic bias without users’ knowledge. Most providers actively instruct their models to conceal these instructions from the public.

    Neumann et al., FAccT 2025; CHI 2026. Read more →
  5. 05
    Financial Services

    The Bank of England Financial Policy Committee has flagged systemic risk from growing AI use in trading, credit scoring, and operational functions, but no AI-specific financial rules exist in the UK.

    Bank of England and PRA, April 2026. Read more →
  6. 06
    Copyright

    The House of Lords described the creative industries as facing a clear and present danger from generative AI. The Government’s March 2026 report acknowledged that no consensus exists on its own objectives.

    Lords Communications Committee, March 2026. Read the briefing →
The United Kingdom’s online-safety framework, designed for a world in which platforms hosted content created by users, is engaging only by extension with a world in which the platform is itself the speaker.

Priority Areas

Where we are focusing our work

The following areas have been identified, through evidence drawn from research, regulatory commentary, and parliamentary inquiry, as priorities for FAIR’s policy and advocacy programme. Additional priorities may be added on formation of the Board.

These remain proposed projects, subject to consultation with and approval by the Board.

AI Copyright and Intellectual Property Reform

A balanced commercial TDM exception with mandatory disclosure, producer rights reform, and a market access provision for models trained outside the UK.

View project →

AI and Child Safety

Comprehensive age gating, content provenance standards, and criminal liability for platform operators of nudification, deepfake, and AI companion services.

View project →

Public Education and AI Literacy

Evidence-based educational programmes and plain-language briefings on every significant AI policy development before Parliament.

View project →

AI Transparency and Accountability

Statutory disclosure obligations covering training data, system instructions, and any commercial arrangements that influence AI outputs.

View project →

Parliamentary Advocacy and Legislative Drafting

Direct engagement with Parliamentarians, expert evidence to Select Committees, model provisions, and cross party work to shape the forthcoming AI Bill.

View project →

Scott McCulloch
Founder, FAIR
Why I Started FAIR
“For nearly twenty years I worked on drug development. The whole point of medicines regulation is that you don’t get to put something into people’s bodies without proving it’s safe and explaining what it does. AI is being put into people’s lives — their hospital appointments, their benefits decisions, their kids’ classrooms — with nothing like that level of care. FAIR is my answer to that gap.”

Scott has spent close to two decades advising drug-development organisations on the regulatory frameworks designed to protect patients in clinical trials. He is in the final stages of an LLB at the University of Law, with postgraduate study in AI Law at the University of Oxford and Intellectual Property at Brunel University London. FAIR is the work he set up because he believes the British public deserves the same rigour of protection from AI that we have long given to medicines — and because the people most affected by AI in this country are not the people currently being asked.
