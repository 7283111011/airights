Why FAIR Exists

AI systems are being deployed at scale into healthcare, education, finance, and public services with no comprehensive safeguards in place.

The UK currently has no AI specific legislation. There are no binding transparency requirements for AI developers. There is no independent audit framework. No statutory obligation exists for developers to disclose what data their models were trained on, what hidden instructions shape their outputs, or what commercial interests influence what users see. The AI (Regulation) Bill remains stalled, and the government has repeatedly deferred action. FAIR was founded to advocate for the enforceable rights and meaningful protections that the UK public deserves.

Built on the principle that innovation and accountability must go together

In industries like pharmaceuticals, aviation, and financial services, innovation operates within rigorous oversight frameworks because the consequences of failure are too serious for voluntary commitments. AI carries comparable risks to individuals and to society, yet it operates in a regulatory vacuum. FAIR exists to make the case, in Parliament and in public, that enforceable standards for AI are both achievable and long overdue.

Rights and Advocacy

Advocating for enforceable individual rights, from the protection of creative works and personal data to safeguards against automated decisions made without human accountability or transparency.

What the evidence shows

Healthcare

NHS clinicians have raised concerns that opaque AI diagnostic tools trained on non representative datasets risk misdiagnosing patients from ethnic minorities, women, and older populations.

Education

Only 9% of UK teachers feel confident teaching AI, yet 76% already use it for daily work. 49% of schools have no AI policy at all. Two thirds of secondary teachers report declining critical thinking among pupils.

NEU Survey, Feb 2026; Pearson School Report, Dec 2025

Child Safety

Ofcom fined an AI nudification site operator in November 2025 and opened investigations into AI chatbot services in January 2026. The proposed KCSIE 2026 guidance formally recognises AI generated abuse material as a safeguarding concern.

Transparency

Research presented at FAccT 2025 and CHI 2026 found that hidden system prompts in AI products introduce systematic bias without users knowledge. Most providers actively instruct their models to conceal these instructions from the public.

Five priority areas

Each project addresses a specific gap in the UK current approach to AI governance.

01
Legislative Priority

AI Copyright and Intellectual Property Reform

Section 29A of the CDPA provides no commercial safe harbour for text and data mining, while rights holders have no meaningful transparency or enforcement tools. FAIR advocates for a balanced commercial TDM exception with mandatory disclosure, producer rights reform, and a market access provision for models trained outside the UK.

Relevant: CDPA 1988 | DSM Directive | EU AI Act | Data (Use and Access) Act 2025
02
Safeguarding

AI and Child Safety

Generative AI has created urgent new risks for children. Nudification tools, deepfake generators, and AI companion chatbots all present documented harms. While the Crime and Policing Bill proposes to ban certain tools, enforcement remains underdeveloped. FAIR will push for comprehensive age gating, content provenance standards, and criminal liability for platform operators.

Relevant: Online Safety Act 2023 | Crime and Policing Bill | KCSIE 2026
03
Public Engagement

Public Education and AI Literacy

Public understanding of AI is critically low. Teachers report having no training or policy guidance. FAIR will develop evidence based educational programmes and publish plain language briefings on every significant AI policy development in Parliament.

Relevant: UK GDPR | Data Protection Act 2018 | Equality Act 2010
04
Technical Standards

AI Transparency and Accountability

The UK has no binding transparency requirements for AI developers. Research has documented that hidden system prompts introduce bias without users knowledge, and that AI products routinely conceal instructions from the people using them. FAIR will advocate for statutory disclosure obligations covering training data, system instructions, and any commercial arrangements that influence AI outputs.

Relevant: EU AI Act (Art. 53) | ICO AI Code of Practice (May 2026)
05
Core Function

Parliamentary Advocacy and Legislative Drafting

The AI (Regulation) Bill remains stalled. The government has deferred comprehensive AI legislation. FAIR will engage directly with Parliamentarians, submit expert evidence to Select Committees, draft model provisions, and work with cross party groups to ensure the forthcoming AI Bill contains enforceable rights and genuine accountability.

Relevant: AI (Regulation) Bill | AI Opportunities Action Plan | King Speech 2026

Why I Started FAIR
"I spent nearly twenty years advising drug development organisations on the navigation of complex, multi jurisdictional regulatory frameworks. AI has no equivalent framework, and the people most affected have had no meaningful say in building one."

For nearly two decades, Scott McCulloch has served as a trusted authority to drug development organisations confronting the full weight of international regulatory obligation. His counsel has guided sponsors, investigators, and service providers through the navigation of multi jurisdictional regulatory frameworks spanning Europe, North America, South America, and the Asia Pacific region, ensuring at every stage that clinical trial data meets the highest standards of integrity, that compliance is not merely aspirational but demonstrable, and that the results upon which regulatory authorities rely in granting market authorisation are robustly defensible. His purview has extended across the entire development lifecycle, from the earliest stages of scientific research through to post authorisation commitments, working hand in hand with some of the world's most exceptional scientists and medical professionals.

It is precisely this sustained, first hand experience of what enforceable regulatory oversight looks like in practice, what it demands of organisations, and what it delivers for the individuals it is designed to protect, that equips Scott McCulloch to lead FAIR.

In concurrence with a distinguished career in the regulated sciences, Scott has undertaken a substantive and deliberate turn toward the law. He is now in the penultimate stages of completing a law degree at the University of Law, alongside preparation for admission as a practising solicitor. He is concurrently reading postgraduate programmes in AI Law at the University of Oxford and Intellectual Property at Brunel University London. He holds a Master of Science in Psychology and Neuroscience from King's College London.

Scott founded FAIR in the hope of applying the same rigour to the use and implementation of artificial intelligence that he has spent his career applying to the protection of patients in clinical trials and in receipt of authorised medicines. He has seen what happens when innovation operates within a framework of enforceable standards: it succeeds, it earns public trust, and it protects the people it touches. The rights of individuals deserve no less than the protections already afforded to patients.
